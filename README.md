Netflix's data often shows that "what critics love" is not "what people actually watch”. The data shows that familiar blockbuster movies aren’t the only media that gets attention. The dataset covers the 10 most popular movies and TV shows each week, overall and by country that Netflix has made publicly available since 2021. While Netflix has been more transparent in their engagement reports, it does not emphasise that international films are having an influence as well. Entertainment has become a sense of comfort in several thousands of people’s homes by coming together to watch movies and tv shows, so having the user's enjoyment reflected accurately is becoming a big deal. Our project focuses on exploring how global audiences engage with blockbuster titles compared to international movies and TV shows, using Netflix’s What We Watched engagement report from January to June 2023.
The story we want to tell is about the gap between what Netflix promotes and what people truly spend time watching. Blockbusters are usually front and center on the platform feed and are assumed to be the most popular because of their marketing and visibility. However, when we look at hours viewed, some international shows and films seem to have stronger staying power and consistent engagement, especially in specific countries. This challenges the idea that only familiar, mainstream content succeeds on Netflix. Because Netflix has started releasing more transparent engagement data, it gives us a chance to better understand what audiences actually enjoy rather than what critics praise or what algorithms push to the top.
Our main idea can be summed up into the theme: global viewing data shows that international content can compete with, and sometimes outperform, blockbuster media in terms of how much people actually watch it. Instead of just looking at rankings or reviews, our project focuses on hours watched to show real audience behavior.
The tension is in the fact that Netflix’s recommendation system often highlights blockbuster content, but viewers don’t always follow those suggestions. Many people choose to watch international shows, foreign-language films, or content that reflects their own culture. This creates a disconnect between what Netflix’s algorithm promotes and what viewers actually value.
Our project also questions a common assumption: that highly praised or heavily marketed content is always what people watch the most. Our intended audience includes Netflix users, students, and anyone curious about media trends. They would care because this project reflects their own viewing habits while showing how global audiences shape streaming culture in ways that aren’t always obvious.
